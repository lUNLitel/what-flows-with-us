Brothers Creek

A creek that I drive by every day without realizing its presence.

Everytime I head to school, it runs with me all the way down the mountain. Then, I will drive across it on a tiny bridge, which I never thought is a bridge.

It flows quietly, unnoticed by most. But it is the life source for the surrounding ecosystem. The bears, the deer, and the birds all depend on it.

It is quiet, yet, powerful. And today, I finally known its name, Brothers Creek.

Unkonwly, Brothers Creek is the third largest watershed in West Vancouver, starting on the southeast side of Hollyburn Ridge. it flows all the way through British Properties, under several roads and the Upper Levels Highway, before joining into the Capilano River.

Apparently, the Brothers Creek was originally known as "Sisters Creek," but confusion with another Capliano tributary of the same name led to it being renamed.

- Ryan

Latimer Creek

I had no idea until I looked at the map how long Latimer Creek ran from Langley Township and into Surrey. I realized that the bus I take almost every day to school and into the city would drive over this watershed. I pass by the pond beside Carvolth Exchange all the time which I just learned is a stormwater pond.

Does Latimer Creek run into this industrialized body of water? Latimer Creek reminded me of Still Creek, as it is salmon-bearing and also cluttered with invasive Himalayan blackberries. Upon learning more about this creek, I'm excited to see how I can better support my community's restoration efforts.

- SG

Fraser River

On my commute, after passing by Latimer Creek, the bus takes us over the Portmann Bridge where, if I look to my left, I see industries, Vancouver, Burnaby, Coquitlam, Surrey, and more, and if I look to my right, I see Douglas Island, Pitt Meadows and a clear view of the mountains (though there is probably still a lot of hidden industrial sites).

This view is a lot different compared to growing up in New Westminster and frequently crossing the former Patullo Bridge (now newly built and called stal̕əw̓asəm) because I don't see the familiar log rafts nor can I feel the breeze of the water's flow because I am further away from water's surface.

- SG

Fraser River

My favourite part of my commute is passing over the Fraser River. Everytime I take the train I look down at Brownsville Bar Park and I count how many ducks are in the water.

I have memories of going to Fraser River with my dad and fishing. We never caught anything but sometimes we would come home and lie to my mom that we caught and released a fish. I don't remember where on the river we would go since it is so huge.

- Olivia

Bear Creek

To get to the Expo Line train my bus drives on King George Blvd. Under and beside the road is Bear Creek. Bear Creek is now a large park with an outdoor pool, football field with a track, skatepark, playground, and some nature trails.

I used to go to Bear Creek a lot as a kid. We would go multiple times a week for my little brothers community football team practices and games. I have memories of walking off and exploring the small streams beside the practice field.

Bear Creek is a salmon and trout bearing stream. I remember seeing fish when visiting the park…I wonder if any were salmon!

- Olivia

Archibald Creek

To get to my first bus exchange I pass over Archibald Creek. I did not realize that I was passing over it. I always thought that I was passing beside it.

I also did not know how large this stream of water is and how connected it is to other streams. It connects to a small creek near my house called McLeod Park. At McLeod Park, I have a lot of animals including a heron and beaver!

I also buried my pet fish there, RIP Peach.

- Olivia

Stoney Creek

When I take the Millennium Line, I pass by Stoney Creek that runs through Burnaby and into the Brunette River and Burnaby Lake. I learned that it is also a salmon-bearing habitat like many other creeks in the Lower Mainland which fills me with a bittersweet feeling that these lands and waters were once plentiful homes for salmon to return to (and there is a lot of work being done to get to that state again).

The "stoney" in its name refers to the stoney stream bed of Burnaby Mountain's largest waterway. As a child, I visited many of these creeks and trails with my family. We no longer do that as often because of different priorities and the general flow of life.

It's weird that most of my interaction with these water bodies is now from above (sometimes way above), fast, and seamless through public transportation. I am eager to visit these waters now and experience immersing myself in the environment and taking slower and more non-linear travels.

- SG

Burrard Inlet in Port Moody

Every time I take the skytrain, I pass by the water body I live closest to and visit every day with my dog, Burrard Inlet in Port Moody. Our interaction is different when I'm in a train watching from afar compared to when I'm right by the shoreline, but the love and appreciation are there either way.

Going through the tunnel in the train after Burquitlam Station and being greeted by the inlet brings me a feeling of coming home and having a familiar face welcoming me back to my neighbourhood.

- Anita Mauriello

Lynn Creek

I drive over Lynn Creek every day to get to school. The mouth of the river is surrounded by industrial infrastructure but, you would never know that up the river a little way houses the most beautiful freshwater oasis I have ever seen.

- Roxanna L Lewon
