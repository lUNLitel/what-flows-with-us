Brothers Creek

A creek that I drive by every day without realizing its presence.

Everytime I head to school, it runs with me all the way down the mountain. Then, I will drive across it on a tiny bridge, which I never thought is a bridge.

It flows quietly, unnoticed by most. But it is the life source for the surrounding ecosystem. The bears, the deer, and the birds all depend on it.

It is quiet, yet, powerful. And today, I finally known its name, Brothers Creek.

- Ryan

Latimer Creek

I had no idea until I looked at the map how long Latimer Creek ran from Langley Township and into Surrey. I realized that the bus I take almost every day to school and into the city would drive over this watershed. I pass by the pond beside Carvolth Exchange all the time which I just learned is a stormwater pond.

Does Latimer Creek run into this industrialized body of water? Latimer Creek reminded me of Still Creek, as it is salmon-bearing and also cluttered with invasive Himalayan blackberries. Upon learning more about this creek, I'm excited to see how I can better support my community's restoration efforts.

- SG

Fraser River

On my commute, after passing by Latimer Creek, the bus takes us over the Portmann Bridge where, if I look to my left, I see industries, Vancouver, Burnaby, Coquitlam, Surrey, and more, and if I look to my right, I see Douglas Island, Pitt Meadows and a clear view of the mountains.

This view is a lot different compared to growing up in New Westminster and frequently crossing the former Patullo Bridge (now newly built and called stal̕əw̓asəm) because I don't see the familiar log rafts nor can I feel the breeze of the water's flow because I am further away from water's surface.

- SG

Stoney Creek

When I take the Millennium Line, I pass by Stoney Creek that runs through Burnaby and into the Brunette River and Burnaby Lake. I learned that it is also a salmon-bearing habitat like many other creeks in the Lower Mainland which fills me with a bittersweet feeling that these lands and waters were once plentiful homes for salmon to return to (and there is a lot of work being done to get to that state again).

The "stoney" in its name refers to the stoney stream bed of Burnaby Mountain's largest waterway. As a child, I visited many of these creeks and trails with my family. We no longer do that as often because of different priorities and the general flow of life.

It's weird that most of my interaction with these water bodies is now from above (sometimes way above), fast, and seamless through public transportation. I am eager to visit these waters now and experience immersing myself in the environment and taking slower and more non-linear travels.

- SG
